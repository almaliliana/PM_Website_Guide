Teams

Building and managing effective project teams in small organisations

Why teams matter in project management

Projects are delivered by people, not plans. In small businesses and charities, you often work with small, multi-functional teams — sometimes volunteers, sometimes part-time staff. Understanding how teams form and perform is essential to project success.

Tuckman (1965) identified four stages of team development: Forming, Storming, Norming and Performing. Most teams experience conflict during the "Storming" phase — this is normal and manageable with strong facilitation.

Building your project team

Start by mapping the skills your project needs, then identify who in your organisation (or network) can fill those roles. In small organisations, people often wear multiple hats — be honest about capacity and avoid overloading key individuals.

  • Define clear roles and responsibilities using a RACI matrix (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed)
  • Hold a project kickoff meeting to establish shared goals and norms
  • Create a team charter outlining how decisions will be made and conflicts resolved
  • Schedule regular check-ins — weekly standups work well for small teams

Managing volunteers and mixed teams

Charities frequently manage teams that include paid staff and volunteers. The PMI (2021) notes that volunteer motivation differs significantly from employee motivation — volunteers are driven by purpose and connection, not financial reward. Recognise contributions publicly, ensure volunteers have meaningful roles, and be flexible about time commitments.

RACI Matrix

Clarifies who does what on every task. Essential for small teams where roles overlap.

Team Charter

A one-page agreement on how the team will work together, communicate and resolve disagreements.

Tuckman Model

Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing. Helps you anticipate team dynamics.

Regular standups

Short daily or weekly check-ins keep small teams aligned without heavy bureaucracy.

Tuckman, B.W. (1965). 'Developmental sequence in small groups', Psychological Bulletin, 63(6), pp. 384–399.

Risk

Identifying, assessing and managing risk in your projects

What is project risk?

A risk is any uncertain event that, if it occurs, could affect your project's objectives. Risks can be positive (opportunities) as well as negative (threats). Effective risk management is not about eliminating risk — it's about making informed decisions about which risks to accept, mitigate or transfer.

The ISO 31000:2018 standard defines risk management as "coordinated activities to direct and control an organisation with regard to risk." It provides a useful, accessible framework for small organisations.

The risk management process

  • Identify: Brainstorm risks with your team. Use a risk register to document them.
  • Assess: Score each risk by likelihood (1–5) and impact (1–5). Multiply for a risk score.
  • Respond: For each significant risk, decide: Avoid, Mitigate, Transfer or Accept.
  • Monitor: Review your risk register regularly throughout the project lifecycle.

The risk register

A risk register is a simple log — often a spreadsheet — that records each identified risk, its likelihood, impact, owner and response plan. Keep it live throughout your project. In small organisations, even a basic register is far better than none at all.

Common risks for Irish small businesses and charities

  • Funding dependency — over-reliance on a single funder or grant
  • Volunteer attrition — key volunteers leaving mid-project
  • Scope creep — project expanding beyond original brief
  • GDPR compliance risk — particularly for charities handling sensitive data
  • Reputational risk — especially relevant for charities with public-facing work

Leadership

Leading projects and people with clarity and purpose

Leadership vs management

Management is about systems and processes; leadership is about people and direction. Kotter (1990) drew a classic distinction: managers cope with complexity, leaders cope with change. As a project lead in a small organisation, you need both — but don't underestimate the human side of the role.

Key leadership styles in project contexts

Transformational

Inspires change through vision and purpose. Works well in charities with a strong mission.

Servant leadership

Prioritises the team's needs. Effective in volunteer-led or community organisations.

Transactional

Reward/penalty based. Useful for clear task delivery but limits creativity.

Situational

Adapts style to context and team maturity. Often most practical for project work.

Emotional intelligence in leadership

Goleman (1995) identified emotional intelligence (EQ) as a critical factor in effective leadership — more important than IQ in many contexts. For project managers leading small teams, self-awareness, empathy and social skills are particularly valuable. EQ allows you to read team dynamics, manage conflict constructively and maintain trust under pressure.

Research by the Chartered Management Institute (CMI, 2023) found that 82% of people who leave jobs cite their manager, not the job itself, as the primary reason. Strong project leadership reduces turnover and increases commitment.

Ethics

Managing projects with integrity and ethical responsibility

Why ethics matter in project management

Ethical project management means making decisions that are fair, transparent, honest and respectful of all stakeholders. For charities, this is especially critical — public trust is a core asset. For small businesses, ethical practice builds lasting reputation and client loyalty.

The PMI Code of Ethics

The Project Management Institute's Code of Ethics (2006) identifies four core values: Responsibility, Respect, Fairness and Honesty. These provide a practical framework for decision-making when facing ethical dilemmas in a project context.

  • Responsibility: Own your decisions and their consequences
  • Respect: Treat team members, clients and stakeholders with dignity
  • Fairness: Be impartial and transparent in decision-making
  • Honesty: Communicate truthfully, even when delivering bad news

Ethical challenges in Irish organisations

Common ethical issues in Irish small business and charity projects include: procurement practices, conflicts of interest on small boards, data privacy (GDPR), fair treatment of volunteers and whistleblowing. The Charities Regulator's Governance Code (2023) sets clear expectations for ethical governance in the sector.

The Speak Up culture — encouraging team members to raise concerns without fear — is now considered a governance best practice. Create a simple informal channel for raising issues early before they escalate.

Sustainability

Embedding environmental and social sustainability into your projects

What is sustainable project management?

Sustainable project management integrates economic, social and environmental considerations into how projects are planned and delivered. The UN's Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs, 2015) provide a widely-used framework for aligning project outcomes with broader societal goals.

The triple bottom line

Elkington's (1997) "triple bottom line" concept — People, Planet, Profit — remains a useful lens. Projects should aim to create value that is socially equitable, environmentally responsible and economically viable. For Irish charities, the "profit" dimension is replaced with long-term financial sustainability.

People

Fair employment, volunteer wellbeing, community benefit and inclusivity in project delivery.

Planet

Reducing waste, cutting carbon, choosing sustainable suppliers and minimising environmental impact.

Profit / Viability

Ensuring the project creates lasting value, not just short-term outputs.

SDGs

Map your project to relevant UN SDGs to demonstrate social and environmental impact to funders.

Practical steps for small organisations

  • Include a sustainability section in your project plan
  • Choose local or Irish suppliers where possible to reduce carbon footprint
  • Measure and report on social impact — not just financial outcomes
  • Consult Green Business Ireland or the EPA's SME guidance for practical tools

Key references

Elkington, J. (1997). Cannibals with Forks: The Triple Bottom Line of 21st Century Business. Oxford: Capstone.

United Nations (2015). Transforming Our World: The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. New York: UN.

GPM Global (2016). The GPM Reference Guide to Sustainability in Project Management. Green Project Management.

EPA Ireland (2023). Green Business Guidance for SMEs. Available at: www.epa.ie

Governance

Structures, accountability and oversight for better project outcomes

What is project governance?

Project governance is the framework of rules, responsibilities and practices that ensure projects are run with appropriate oversight and accountability. It answers the question: "Who decides what, and how?" Good governance prevents scope creep, budget overruns and unethical practices.

Governance in Irish charities

The Charities Regulator's Governance Code (2023) is the primary reference for Irish charitable organisations. It sets out five principles: Advancing the charitable purpose; Operating with integrity; Leading the charity effectively; Exercising control; and Being accountable and transparent. These principles apply directly to how projects within charities should be governed.

For small charities, good governance doesn't require bureaucracy. A clear project brief, a named decision-maker, a simple reporting structure and documented minutes are often sufficient.

Key governance structures for projects

  • Project Sponsor: Senior person accountable for the project's success
  • Project Manager: Day-to-day delivery lead
  • Steering Group: Oversight body that reviews progress and approves key decisions
  • Project Board: In PRINCE2, the formal governance body comprising sponsor, senior user and senior supplier

Strategy

Connecting your project to your organisation's bigger picture

Strategic alignment

Every project should serve a strategic purpose. Before beginning any project, ask: how does this contribute to our organisational mission and goals? Projects that lack strategic alignment often lose momentum, struggle to secure resources and fail to deliver lasting value.

Tools for strategic analysis

SWOT Analysis

Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. Useful for assessing organisational readiness for a project.

PESTLE Analysis

Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental. Maps the external environment affecting your project.

Logic Model

Links inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes. Widely used by Irish funders to evaluate project proposals.

Balanced Scorecard

Tracks performance across financial, customer, internal process and learning dimensions (Kaplan & Norton, 1996).

Strategy in small organisations

Small businesses and charities often operate without formal strategy documents. Mintzberg (1994) distinguished between deliberate strategies (planned) and emergent strategies (evolved through practice). In small organisations, strategy is often emergent — and that's fine, as long as there is enough direction to prioritise projects and allocate resources effectively.

Key references

Mintzberg, H. (1994). The Rise and Fall of Strategic Planning. New York: Free Press.

Kaplan, R.S. and Norton, D.P. (1996). The Balanced Scorecard. Boston: Harvard Business School Press.

Johnson, G., Whittington, R. and Scholes, K. (2020). Exploring Strategy. 12th edn. Harlow: Pearson.

Enterprise Ireland (2023). Strategic Planning for SMEs. Available at: www.enterprise-ireland.com

Planning

From project brief to workable plan, step by step

Why planning matters

The Standish Group's CHAOS Report (2020) found that poor planning is the leading cause of project failure globally. Yet many small organisations skip or rush planning in favour of "just getting started." A well-structured plan does not need to be complex — it needs to be honest, realistic and shared.

The project planning process

  • Define scope: What is included and excluded? Agree this in writing with your sponsor.
  • Break it down: Use a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) to decompose deliverables into tasks.
  • Estimate time and cost: Involve the people doing the work in estimation — they know best.
  • Sequence tasks: Identify dependencies using a simple Gantt chart or task board.
  • Allocate resources: Assign named owners to each task.
  • Plan for risk: Build contingency time and budget into your plan from the start.
A useful rule of thumb: your plan is wrong. That's okay. The value of planning is not the document — it's the thinking. Review and update your plan regularly as you learn more.

Free planning tools for small organisations

You don't need expensive software. Trello (free), Asana (free tier), Google Sheets and even a whiteboard can serve most small project planning needs. The best tool is the one your team will actually use.

Implementation

Delivering your project on time, on budget and to scope

The delivery phase

Implementation is where plans meet reality. This is the longest and most unpredictable phase of most projects. The key disciplines are: consistent monitoring and control, clear communication with stakeholders, managing scope change and maintaining team motivation.

Monitor and control

Use a simple status report (weekly or fortnightly) to track progress against plan. The Earned Value Management (EVM) approach — comparing planned vs actual work and cost — is used in large projects but the underlying principle (is the work on track?) applies to all projects regardless of size.

Status reporting

RAG (Red / Amber / Green) status on time, cost, scope and risk. Simple and effective.

Change control

Any change to scope, budget or timeline should be documented and approved. Prevents scope creep.

Issue log

Record issues as they arise with owner and resolution date. Different from risks — issues have already occurred.

Lessons learned

Document what worked and what didn't throughout delivery, not just at the end.

Project closure

Close your project formally. This means: confirming deliverables have been accepted, completing a lessons-learned review, archiving documentation, releasing team members and communicating outcomes to stakeholders. Closure is often skipped but is essential for organisational learning.

PMI (2021). PMBOK Guide. 7th edn. Project Management Institute.

AXELOS (2017). Managing Successful Projects with PRINCE2. 6th edn.

Kerzner, H. (2022). Project Management. 13th edn. Wiley.

Stakeholders

Identifying and engaging the people who matter to your project

Who are stakeholders?

A stakeholder is anyone who affects or is affected by your project. For Irish small businesses and charities, stakeholders typically include: funders, board members, staff, volunteers, service users, partner organisations, local authorities and the broader community.

Stakeholder mapping

Freeman's (1984) stakeholder theory established that organisations must account for all those with a stake in their activities. The classic power-interest grid divides stakeholders into four quadrants: Manage closely (high power, high interest), Keep satisfied (high power, low interest), Keep informed (low power, high interest), Monitor (low power, low interest).

In charities, service users are often low-power but high-interest stakeholders. Ethical project management means prioritising their voice even when they lack formal power to influence decisions.

Stakeholder engagement plan

  • Identify all stakeholders at the start of the project
  • Assess each stakeholder's power, interest and attitude (supportive / neutral / resistant)
  • Define your engagement approach for each: inform, consult or collaborate
  • Review and update throughout the project as stakeholder positions change

Freeman, R.E. (1984). Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach. Boston: Pitman.

Bourne, L. (2015). Making Projects Work: Effective Stakeholder and Communication Management. Boca Raton: CRC Press.

Methodologies

Choosing the right approach for your project

Why methodology matters

A project methodology is a structured set of principles and practices for managing projects. Choosing the right one for your context improves predictability, communication and outcomes. The wrong methodology — or no methodology — is a common source of project failure.

Waterfall

Linear, sequential phases. Best for projects with fixed scope and clear requirements. Common in construction and IT infrastructure.

Agile

Iterative, flexible delivery in short sprints. Best for projects where requirements evolve. Common in software, product and service design.

PRINCE2

Process-based UK methodology. Strong on governance and roles. Popular in public sector and larger organisations. Free Foundation training available.

Hybrid

Combines waterfall planning with agile delivery. Increasingly common in small organisations that need structure but also flexibility.

Which methodology suits small organisations?

For most Irish small businesses and charities, a lightweight hybrid approach works best. Use waterfall-style planning (clear scope, timeline and budget) with agile-style delivery (short iterations, regular reviews and flexibility to adapt). Full PRINCE2 implementation can be too heavy for small teams, but its principles — business case, defined roles, managed stages — are universally valuable.

AI in project management

How artificial intelligence is changing how projects are planned and delivered

AI and the project manager

Artificial intelligence is increasingly embedded in project management tools. From automated scheduling and risk prediction to natural language reporting and meeting transcription, AI offers significant productivity gains — particularly for small teams with limited administrative capacity.

Practical AI tools for small organisations

  • Meeting summaries: Tools like Otter.ai and Microsoft Copilot automatically transcribe and summarise meetings
  • Task automation: Asana, Monday.com and ClickUp use AI to suggest task prioritisation and flag risks
  • Document drafting: AI writing assistants can produce first drafts of project plans, reports and stakeholder updates
  • Data analysis: AI tools can identify patterns in project data — budget variances, delivery delays — faster than manual review

Risks and ethical considerations

The use of AI in project management raises important questions. The EU AI Act (2024) introduces regulatory requirements for AI systems used in high-risk contexts. For charities and public-facing organisations, data privacy (GDPR), algorithmic bias and transparency are key concerns. Shenhar and Dvir (2007) argue that projects are fundamentally human endeavours — AI should augment, not replace, human judgement.

The PMI's AI in Project Management report (2023) found that 21% of project managers already use AI regularly, and over 80% expect significant AI integration within five years. Understanding AI is now a core project management skill.
